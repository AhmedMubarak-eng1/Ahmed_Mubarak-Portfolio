McMaster Automation Learning Factory – Multiple Projects

McMaster University – W Booth School 2024 – 2025 Hamilton, Ontario

Overview

As an Automation Lab Research Assistant at McMaster University's W Booth School of Engineering Practice and Technology, I contributed to multiple hands-on automation and research projects within the SEPT Learning Factory — a real-world industrial automation environment equipped with Allen-Bradley PLCs, HMIs, VFDs, conveyors, pneumatic systems, and robotics.

These projects spanned PLC programming and HMI development, pneumatic control systems, RC vehicle automation with advanced electronics, and humanitarian engineering research — all while simultaneously teaching Allen-Bradley PLC courses to undergraduate students as a Teaching Assistant.

01 Conveyor System – PLC & HMI Upgrade

What Was Done

Upgraded a FlexLink conveyor belt control system to improve reliability, operator usability, and fault handling. This involved reprogramming an Allen-Bradley PLC and rebuilding the operator interface in FactoryTalk View — delivering a production-quality HMI mimic screen showing the full conveyor layout with live state indication, spur control, and Start/Stop/Reset operator controls.

FactoryTalk View HMI — FlexLink Conveyor Overview Mimic showing live Running state, spur zone control, and operator Start/Stop/Reset

Scope of Work

Modified PLC ladder logic to improve conveyor sequencing, spur routing, and fault handling. Upgraded HMI screens to show real-time zone status, conveyor state (Aborted / Stopped / Idle / Paused / Running / Held / Complete), and operator alarm visibility.

Also supported wiring verification and I/O checks — ensuring all sensors, VFDs, and motor starters were correctly integrated before go-live in the teaching lab.

02 RC Car Platform & Driver Seat Force Simulation

What Was Done

Contributed to two interconnected research platforms — an instrumented RC car with embedded electronics for autonomous and remote control research, and a driver seat force simulation rig that used PLC-controlled pneumatic cylinders to physically simulate braking and acceleration forces on the driver.

My focus was on the PLC control logic for the pneumatic actuators — designing the sequencing logic to synchronize actuator motion with simulated vehicle dynamics in real time, and supporting troubleshooting of pneumatic circuits and timing during commissioning.

RC car platform with embedded control electronics (left) and driver seat force simulation rig with PLC-controlled pneumatic actuators (right)

Full Scope of Work

  • Upgraded FlexLink conveyor Allen-Bradley PLC logic — improved sequencing, fault handling, and spur zone routing
  • Rebuilt FactoryTalk View HMI screens — full conveyor mimic with live state, spur control, and alarm visibility for the teaching lab
  • Supported wiring verification, I/O checks, and VFD integration for the conveyor motor control system
  • Designed and programmed PLC control logic for pneumatic actuators in the driver seat force simulation rig — synchronizing actuator motion with simulated vehicle dynamics
  • Contributed to the RC car platform build — integrating embedded electronics including Raspberry Pi, servo controllers, camera module, and wireless communication
  • Supported troubleshooting of pneumatic circuits and timing during commissioning of the simulation rig
  • Contributed to 3D-printed water filtration research for deployment in Uganda — prototyping, testing, and producing assembly documentation for local manufacturing
  • Supported assembly and field testing of a smart walking cane for seniors — integrating sensors for weight, temperature, heart rate, and weather display across six deployed prototypes

Results & Impact

Delivered a modernized, production-quality conveyor control system used daily in McMaster's teaching lab — improving the learning experience for students in Allen-Bradley PLC and automation courses. The driver seat simulation rig provided a hands-on research platform for vehicle dynamics study, and the RC car platform contributed to ongoing autonomous systems research at the university.

Beyond the automation work, contributions to the Uganda water filtration project and smart walking cane program demonstrated applied engineering in humanitarian and healthcare contexts — delivering six working assistive device prototypes deployed in a real seniors' home.
